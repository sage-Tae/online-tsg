\section*{Highlights}
\begin{itemize}
\item Online TSG: coalitions restricted to feasibility under arrival dynamics.
\item Temporal Nucleolus via sequential LP over the feasibility family $\F$.
\item Sufficient emptiness bound $r>r^{\ast\ast}$, tight for complement coalitions.
\item Structural safeguard $k<n-1$ blocks the complement-coalition mechanism.
\item Asymptotic tightening $r^{\ast\ast}\to 1$ at $O(n^{-1/2})$ confirmed to $n=50$.
\end{itemize}

\begin{abstract}
\noindent We introduce the \emph{Online Traveling Salesman Game} (Online TSG), a cooperative game in which customers arrive over time and cost must be allocated only among coalitions realizable under the revealed arrival dynamics. The classical static TSG is recovered at simultaneous arrivals. We formalize the family $\F$ of feasible coalitions, define the Temporal Nucleolus as the lexicographic minimizer of excess restricted to $\F$, and give a sequential LP procedure to compute it. Four theoretical results address Core stability under dynamics: (i) the Temporal Nucleolus reduces to the static Nucleolus at simultaneous arrivals; (ii) $r > r^{\ast\ast}$ with $r^{\ast\ast}=1+\min_{i:\,N\setminus\{i\}\in\F} \delta_i/\cstar(N)$ (when applicable) is sufficient for Core emptiness via the complement-coalition mechanism; (iii) a bounded peak queue $k<n-1$ structurally blocks that mechanism; and (iv) $r^{\ast\ast}\to 1$ almost surely at rate $O(n^{-1/2})$ under uniform sampling, tying the cooperative-game threshold to the Beardwood--Halton--Hammersley $\sqrt{n}$ scaling of the Euclidean TSP. Over 175 random instances with $n\in\{5,7,10,12,15\}$ and seven arrival patterns, the sufficient condition fires correctly in 14 of 14 applicable empty-Core cases, and every instance with $k<n-1$ (79 of 79) retains a nonempty Core. The complement-mechanism threshold $\bar r^{\ast\ast}=1.355$ is sharper than the classical bound $\bar r^{\ast}=4.351$ by a factor of $3.21$, and scale-up experiments to $n=50$ confirm that the structural safeguard holds. Our empirical analysis identifies three mechanisms affecting Core stability: the complement-coalition mechanism (Theorem 11), the structural obstruction at bounded queue depth (Corollary 16), and an empirically observed near-complement mechanism (Remark 14) certified by a restricted Core LP when simultaneous binding of size-$(n-1)$ and size-$(n-2)$ coalitions rules out the Core without any single complement violation.
\end{abstract}

\noindent\textbf{Keywords:} Game theory; Online optimization; Cooperative game theory; Traveling salesman game; Nucleolus; Core stability; Dynamic routing.

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:introduction}

Urban last-mile logistics is characterized by requests that enter the system \emph{after} planning begins: online grocery orders, same-day parcels, ride-hail queues and crowdsourced pickups all share the feature that the identity of who will be served together with whom is not known until dispatch time \citep{pillac2013review,ausiello2001oltsp}. Operators nevertheless must share the routing cost among the customers who are eventually served on a joint tour, and the quality of this allocation shapes incentives for participation and pricing \citep{guajardo2016review}. Cooperative game theory, and specifically the Traveling Salesman Game (TSG) of \citet{potters1992tsg}, supplies the canonical framework in which a tour cost is divided so that no subset of customers has a strict incentive to defect; the Vehicle Routing Game (VRG) of \citet{gothe1996vrg,engevall2004heterogeneous} extends the idea to multi-vehicle settings. Classic applications of the VRG include school bus routing \citep{park2012schoolbus}, where the set of customers is fixed before the vehicle departs. Online counterparts arise naturally in same-day delivery and ride-hailing platforms, where requests arrive dynamically and the coalition of customers to be served jointly is revealed only during execution.

The classical TSG, however, is defined under the tacit assumption that every customer is available to be visited at any point of the tour: any coalition $S\subseteq N$ can be \emph{imagined} as a self-contained instance, and its characteristic cost $c(S)$ is the optimal TSP tour on $S\cup\{0\}$. In online settings, this counterfactual is not available: a coalition $S$ is operationally meaningful only if there exists a moment at which all members of $S$ have arrived but none have yet been served. Ignoring temporality hides a central source of instability. Two customers who arrive far apart in time are never candidates to be served together, and pretending that they form a coalition distorts fairness. Recent work on online cooperative games \citep{ge2024incentives,aziz2025participation,goyal2025temporal} has begun to address temporality but not in the routing context.

\paragraph{Research question.} What is the correct cooperative game-theoretic abstraction of the online traveling salesman problem, how does it compute, and---most importantly---when does it admit a stable allocation?

\paragraph{Contributions.} We make four contributions.
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.5em]
\item[\textbf{C1.}] \emph{Model.} We define the Online TSG $(N, c, \F)$ by restricting cost sharing to the family $\F$ of coalitions feasible under the realized arrival times, and we introduce the Temporal Nucleolus as the lexicographic minimizer of excess over $\F$. The classical static TSG is recovered as the special case where arrivals collapse to a single instant (Theorem~\ref{thm:static-equiv}).
\item[\textbf{C2.}] \emph{Core theory under dynamics.} We provide a sufficient condition for empty Core based on an arrival-aware ratio $r^{\ast\ast}$, tight relative to the complement-coalition mechanism (Theorem~\ref{thm:empty-core}), paired with a structural obstruction: a bounded peak queue $k = \max_t |U_t| < n-1$ blocks that mechanism (Corollary~\ref{cor:cor52}). Together these delineate the regimes in which complement-coalition instability can arise. A third, near-complement mechanism (Remark~\ref{rem:near-complement}) explains the empirical minority of empty-Core instances at $r\le r^{\ast\ast}$ through simultaneous binding of $(n-1)$- and $(n-2)$-coalitions; its analytic characterization is left open.
\item[\textbf{C3.}] \emph{Asymptotic refinement.} Under uniform coordinate sampling, $r^{\ast\ast}\to 1$ at rate $O(n^{-1/2})$ almost surely (Theorem~\ref{thm:asymptotic}); this explains the empirical smallness of $r^{\ast\ast}$ without reference to specific simulation parameters and ties the cooperative-game threshold to the classical Beardwood--Halton--Hammersley $\sqrt{n}$ scaling of the Euclidean TSP.
\item[\textbf{C4.}] \emph{Empirical calibration.} On 175 simulated instances across five coalition sizes and seven arrival patterns under three dispatch policies (525 policy-instance pairs), plus 45 scale-up instances up to $n=50$ and a 24-instance scale-invariance study (Appendix~\ref{app:scale-invariance}), we verify every theoretical claim. The complement-mechanism threshold fires correctly in $14/14$ applicable empty-Core cases under nearest-neighbor dispatch (with no false positives under any policy); the structural obstruction holds in $79/79$ instances in which it applies; the mean threshold ($1.355$) is sharper than the classical bound ($4.351$) by a factor of $3.21$; and the asymptotic decay $r^{\ast\ast}\to 1$ is consistent with Theorem~\ref{thm:asymptotic}'s $O(n^{-1/2})$ rate on the scale-up grid.
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Practical implication.} In operational terms, our results say that an online dispatch regime with fully sequential arrivals ($k<n-1$) is \emph{automatically} core-stable, whereas the pathological regime is the ``morning batch'' in which many orders sit simultaneously in the queue. This aligns with the observation, ubiquitous in practice, that same-day delivery platforms enforce short service windows precisely to keep the queue shallow.

\paragraph{Structure.} Section~\ref{sec:related} situates our work within the literature on static TSG/VRG, dynamic and rolling-horizon games, online cooperative games, and nucleolus computation. Section~\ref{sec:problem} formalizes the Online TSG. Section~\ref{sec:nucleolus} defines and computes the Temporal Nucleolus. Section~\ref{sec:theory} develops the Core theory. Section~\ref{sec:experiments} reports computational experiments, and Section~\ref{sec:conclusion} concludes.

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

We organize prior work along four streams and state, for each, the precise point of departure of our contribution.

\subsection{Static Traveling Salesman and Vehicle Routing Games}
The cooperative TSP was initiated by \citet{potters1992tsg} with a characteristic function equal to the optimal TSP tour cost on the chosen coalition. \citet{tamir1989core} showed that the Core may be empty even on the Euclidean line, and the gap between fair and feasible allocations was quantified via approximation in \citet{faigle1998approximate} and \citet{blaser2008metric}. In parallel the Vehicle Routing Game (VRG) received a Nucleolus-based treatment by \citet{gothe1996vrg}, extended to heterogeneous fleets by \citet{engevall2004heterogeneous} and to time-window settings by \citet{tae2020vrgtw} and \citet{arroyo2024vrptw}. The Shapley value formulation of cooperative TSP under rolling horizons is treated by \citet{kimms2016rolling}, and a core-based variant by \citet{kimms2016core}. A broad survey is given by \citet{guajardo2016review}. \emph{Departure.} Every cited study defines coalitions over the full customer set $2^N$: coalition identity is assumed known \emph{a priori}. We replace $2^N$ with the data-dependent family $\F$ of coalitions realizable under the online arrival process.

\subsection{Dynamic and Rolling-Horizon Cooperative Games}
\citet{kranich2005dynamic} proposed Core concepts for TU games played over discrete time. \citet{habis2010weak} defined the weak sequential Core to accommodate renegotiation, and \citet{lehrer2013dynamic} studied how the Core evolves as information unfolds. \citet{kimms2016rolling} applied rolling horizons to cooperative TSP, redistributing cost as new customers enter. \emph{Departure.} These works abstract away the physical routing cost structure: coalitions are described combinatorially with exogenous values $v(S,t)$. In contrast we retain the metric TSP cost $c(S)$ and let temporality enter through the \emph{admissibility} of coalitions, which couples the game to the dispatch policy.

\subsection{Online Cooperative Games}
\citet{bullinger2023online} studied online coalition formation under random arrival and dissolution. \citet{ge2024incentives} and \citet{zhang2025incentives} designed incentives for early arrival in online cost-sharing settings. \citet{aziz2025participation} characterized participation incentives for players entering over time. \citet{goyal2025temporal} introduce Temporal Cooperative Games, in which the characteristic function is indexed by time. \emph{Departure.} These models differ from ours in two respects. First, \citet{bullinger2023online} formalize coalition \emph{formation} (which players group with whom) rather than cost sharing within a fixed grand coalition, so their stability concepts and ours address distinct strategic problems. Second, and common to the whole stream, none of these models equip the online coalition process with a TSP or routing cost structure; the inequalities that define our Temporal Core reflect exactly the geometric interactions among customers in the plane, allowing the sharp thresholds $r^{\ast\ast}$ and structural bound $k<n-1$ established in Section~\ref{sec:theory}.

\subsection{Nucleolus Computation}
\citet{schmeidler1969nucleolus} introduced the Nucleolus as the lexicographic minimizer of the excess vector. The sequential-LP approach that we adopt descends from Kohlberg's algorithm and its careful modernization by \citet{guajardo2015nucleolus}, with further scaling to large games by \citet{nguyen2016large}. Nucleolus-based ridesharing is treated in \citet{lu2019ridesharing}, and Nucleolus computation for the VRG with time windows (VRPTW) in \citet{tae2020vrgtw}. \emph{Departure.} Our LP cascade is defined over $\F$ rather than $2^N\setminus\{\emptyset\}$: in sequential arrival regimes this can reduce the per-LP constraint count by more than an order of magnitude (Section~\ref{sec:experiments}), keeping the method computationally viable for realistic problem sizes.

A separate line of theoretical work relevant to our asymptotic analysis (Theorem~\ref{thm:asymptotic}) is the probabilistic theory of Euclidean combinatorial optimization, initiated by \citet{beardwood1959shortest} and systematized by \citet{steele1981subadditive,steele1997probability}. The Beardwood--Halton--Hammersley theorem characterizes the $\sqrt{n}$ scaling of the optimal Euclidean TSP tour, which in our setting controls the denominator of $r^{\ast\ast}$ and hence its asymptotic tightening. To the best of our knowledge, this asymptotic perspective on cooperative routing games is new: prior cooperative TSP and VRG studies \citep{potters1992tsg,gothe1996vrg,engevall2004heterogeneous,kimms2016core} treat the coalition cost as a fixed quantity rather than as a random variable with a known limit distribution.

\label{sec:problem}

\subsection{Notation}

\subsection{Feasible coalitions and the characteristic function}

The starting point is that the characteristic cost $c(S)$ of a coalition is \emph{defined in the same way as in the static TSG}: it is the cost of the optimal TSP tour on $S\cup\{0\}$ irrespective of time. Temporality enters only through the admissibility of coalitions.

\begin{definition}[Common waiting window]
For a coalition $S\subseteq N$,
$\CW(S) := \bigcap_{i\in S} [a_i, s_i)$.
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Feasible coalition]
\label{def:feasible}
A nonempty coalition $S\subseteq N$ is \emph{feasible} under the realized arrival/service sequence $(a, s)$ if $\CW(S)\neq\emptyset$, equivalently if there exists an event time $t$ at which $S\subseteq U_t$. Let
$\F := \{S\subseteq N : \CW(S)\neq\emptyset\}$
denote the family of feasible coalitions.
\end{definition}

Note that $\{i\}\in\F$ for every $i$ (singletons are always feasible at $a_i$) and $N\in\F$ whenever all $n$ customers sit simultaneously in the queue at some instant.

\begin{definition}[Online TSG]
\label{def:online-tsg}
An \emph{Online Traveling Salesman Game} is a triple $(N, c, \F)$ where $N$ is the customer set, $c:2^N\setminus\{\emptyset\}\to\mathbb{R}_+$ is the TSP characteristic cost defined on every nonempty subset (and used in core constraints only for $S\in\F$), and $\F\subseteq 2^N\setminus\{\emptyset\}$ is the family from Definition~\ref{def:feasible}.
\end{definition}

An \emph{allocation} is a vector $x\in\mathbb{R}^n$ with $\sum_i x_i = C(N)_{\text{online}}$, where $C(N)_{\text{online}}$ denotes the realized online tour cost. The \emph{Temporal Core} is
\[
\mathrm{Core}(N,c,\F) := \bigl\{ x\in\mathbb{R}^n : \textstyle\sum_i x_i = C(N)_{\text{online}},\; \sum_{i\in S} x_i \le c(S)\ \forall S\in\F \bigr\}.
\]
In the static boundary case $C(N)_{\text{online}} = \cstar(N) = c(N)$, and the classical \citet{potters1992tsg} definition is recovered. Compared with the static TSG, the Temporal Core imposes fewer inequalities---only those for $S\in\F$. Table~\ref{tab:static-vs-temporal} summarizes the contrast.

\paragraph{Characteristic cost versus realized cost.} The characteristic function $c(S)$ is the time-independent optimal TSP tour on $S\cup\{0\}$, matching the classical \citet{potters1992tsg} definition. The realized online tour cost $C(N)_{\text{online}}$, by contrast, is the total travel distance of the tour actually executed by the online dispatch policy, which may exceed $\cstar(N)$ because the online policy cannot anticipate future arrivals; in general $C(N)_{\text{online}} \ge \cstar(N)$. We use $\sum_i x_i = C(N)_{\text{online}}$ as the efficiency budget because this is the cost the platform must actually allocate; the competitive ratio $r = C(N)_{\text{online}}/\cstar(N)$ measures the overhead introduced by online dispatch, since the online policy must commit to visits without seeing future arrivals. The threshold $r^{\ast\ast}$ (Theorem~\ref{thm:empty-core}) is a function of coordinate geometry alone through $c(\{i\})$, $c(N\setminus\{i\})$, and $\cstar(N)$.

\section{The Temporal Nucleolus}
\label{sec:nucleolus}

Even when the Core is nonempty it is typically a polytope, and one needs a selection rule. We adopt the nucleolus of \citet{schmeidler1969nucleolus}, adapted to the feasible family $\F$.

\subsection{Definition}
For an allocation $x$ and $S\in\F$, the \emph{excess} is $e(S, x) := \sum_{i\in S} x_i - c(S)$. Let $\theta(x)$ be the vector $(e(S,x))_{S\in\F}$ sorted in non-increasing order.

\begin{definition}[Temporal Nucleolus]
\label{def:tnu}
The Temporal Nucleolus of $(N, c, \F)$ is
\[
\mathrm{TNu}(N,c,\F) := \arg\!\min{}^{\text{lex}} \bigl\{ \theta(x) : \textstyle\sum_i x_i = C(N)_{\text{online}}\bigr\},
\]
where the minimization is lexicographic over the sorted excess vector.
\end{definition}

\subsection{Sequential LP computation}

Following the standard technique of \citet{guajardo2015nucleolus}, the Temporal Nucleolus is computed by solving at most $|\F|$ linear programs. At stage $q$ the LP
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:lp-q}
\begin{aligned}
\min_{x,\varepsilon} \;& \varepsilon \\
\text{s.t. } \;& \sum_{i} x_i = C(N)_{\text{online}}, \\
& \sum_{i\in S} x_i - c(S) \le \varepsilon \qquad \forall S\in\F\setminus\F_{<q}, \\
& \sum_{i\in S} x_i - c(S) = \varepsilon_{q(S)}^{\ast} \qquad \forall S\in\F_{<q},
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}
determines the $q$-th largest coordinate of $\theta$. Here $\F_{<q}$ collects the coalitions whose excess was fixed at earlier stages with optimal value $\varepsilon_{q(S)}^{\ast}$. Finiteness of $\F$ guarantees termination in at most $|\F|$ stages; each LP has $n+1$ variables and $|\F|+1$ constraints.

The restriction from $2^N\setminus\{\emptyset\}$ to $\F$ materially reduces problem size whenever arrivals are spread in time. Section~\ref{sec:experiments} reports empirically $|\F|/(2^n-1)$ as low as $0.061$ for heavily sequential patterns.

\subsection{Reduction to the static game at simultaneous arrivals}

\begin{theorem}[Static TSG as a boundary case]
\label{thm:static-equiv}
If $a_1 = a_2 = \cdots = a_n$ and $s_i > a_1$ for every $i\in N$, then $\F = 2^N\setminus\{\emptyset\}$ and the Temporal Nucleolus coincides with the static Nucleolus of \citet{potters1992tsg}.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
By assumption there exists $t^{\star}\in[a_1,\min_i s_i)$ with $U_{t^{\star}} = N$. Every $S\subseteq N$ satisfies $S\subseteq U_{t^{\star}}$, so $\CW(S)\supseteq [a_1,\min_i s_i)\neq\emptyset$ and $\F = 2^N\setminus\{\emptyset\}$. The Temporal Nucleolus LP cascade~\eqref{eq:lp-q} reduces to the standard Kohlberg sequence for the TSG, whose fixed point is the static Nucleolus. Since lexicographic minimization is deterministic, the two coincide.
\end{proof}

In short, the Online TSG is a proper generalization: the static theory is the $k=n$ extremal case where the peak queue contains everybody at once.

\section{Theoretical Results}
\label{sec:theory}

We now develop the Core theory. Section~\ref{ssec:empty} establishes conditions under which the Temporal Core is \emph{empty}; Section~\ref{ssec:survive} gives conditions under which it is guaranteed \emph{nonempty}. Section~\ref{ssec:discussion} discusses the relationships between the parameters $r$, $r^{\ast\ast}$, $k$, and the queue dynamics.

\subsection{Conditions under which the Core vanishes}
\label{ssec:empty}

\begin{remark}[Necessary super-additivity failure]
\label{rem:remark1}
If the online tour obeys $C(N)_{\text{online}} > \sum_{i\in N} c(\{i\})$, then any allocation summing to $C(N)_{\text{online}}$ violates the singleton constraints $\sum_{\{i\}} x_i \le c(\{i\})$, so the Temporal Core is empty. This is only a \emph{loose} condition; in our experiments it almost never fires because $C(N)_{\text{online}}/\cstar(N) < \sum_i c(\{i\})/\cstar(N) = r^{\ast}$ by a large margin.
\end{remark}

\begin{remark}[Remark~\ref{rem:remark1} reinterpreted as a ratio]
\label{rem:rstar}
Dividing both sides by $\cstar(N)$, Remark~\ref{rem:remark1} is equivalent to $r > r^{\ast}$, where $r^{\ast} := \sum_i c(\{i\})/\cstar(N)$. Because $r^{\ast}$ is typically in the range $3$--$4$ while the competitive ratio of reasonable online policies is close to $1$, this test is rarely tight.
\end{remark}

We sharpen Remark~\ref{rem:remark1} substantially by exploiting complement coalitions of the form $N\setminus\{i\}$.

\begin{lemma}[Feasibility of $N\setminus\{i\}$]
\label{lem:lemma51}
For each $i\in N$, $N\setminus\{i\}\in\F$ if and only if $\max_{j\neq i} a_j < \min_{j\neq i} s_j$.
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
($\Rightarrow$) If $N\setminus\{i\}\in\F$ there is $t$ with $N\setminus\{i\}\subseteq U_t$. Then $a_j\le t < s_j$ for every $j\neq i$, so $\max_j a_j \le t < \min_j s_j$ (both over $j\neq i$). ($\Leftarrow$) Conversely, if $\max_{j\neq i} a_j < \min_{j\neq i} s_j$, any $t$ in the open interval between them satisfies $a_j\le t<s_j$ for all $j\neq i$, so $N\setminus\{i\}\subseteq U_t$ and $N\setminus\{i\}\in\F$.
\end{proof}

\begin{lemma}[Fully sequential arrivals preclude $N\setminus\{i\}\in\F$]
\label{lem:lemma52}
If arrivals are fully sequential in the sense that $s_j \le a_{j+1}$ for all $j=1,\ldots,n-1$ (each customer is served before the next arrives), then $N\setminus\{i\}\notin\F$ for every $i\in N$.
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
Fix $i$ and assume customers are indexed in arrival order $1,\ldots,n$ so that $a_1 \le \cdots \le a_n$. Let $\ell^{\star}=\max\{j:j\neq i\}$ and $j^{\star}=\min\{j:j\neq i\}$ denote, respectively, the latest and earliest arrival-order indices other than $i$. By the sequential-arrival hypothesis $s_j \le a_{j+1}$ applied along the chain $j^{\star}, j^{\star}+1, \ldots, \ell^{\star}-1$, we obtain $s_{j^{\star}} \le a_{j^{\star}+1} \le s_{j^{\star}+1} \le \cdots \le a_{\ell^{\star}}$, hence $a_{\ell^{\star}} \ge s_{j^{\star}}$. Therefore $\max_{j\neq i} a_j \ge \min_{j\neq i} s_j$, violating the if-and-only-if criterion of Lemma~\ref{lem:lemma51}.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[Sufficient condition for empty Temporal Core via complement coalitions]
\label{thm:empty-core}
Assume that $N\setminus\{i\}\in\F$ for some $i\in N$. Let
\[
\delta_i := c(\{i\}) + c(N\setminus\{i\}) - \cstar(N),\qquad r^{\ast\ast}:= 1 + \min_{i:\,N\setminus\{i\}\in\F} \delta_i/\cstar(N).
\]
If $r = C(N)_{\text{online}}/\cstar(N) > r^{\ast\ast}$, then the Temporal Core is empty.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Let $i^{\star}\in\arg\min_{i:\,N\setminus\{i\}\in\F} \delta_i$. Both $\{i^{\star}\}\in\F$ (singletons are always feasible) and $N\setminus\{i^{\star}\}\in\F$. Suppose, for contradiction, that $x$ lies in the Temporal Core. The two core inequalities read
\begin{align}
x_{i^{\star}} &\le c(\{i^{\star}\}), \label{eq:sing}\\
\sum_{j\neq i^{\star}} x_j &\le c(N\setminus\{i^{\star}\}).\label{eq:comp}
\end{align}
Adding~\eqref{eq:sing} and~\eqref{eq:comp} gives $\sum_{j\in N} x_j \le c(\{i^{\star}\}) + c(N\setminus\{i^{\star}\}) = \cstar(N) + \delta_{i^{\star}}$. But the efficiency constraint forces $\sum_j x_j = C(N)_{\text{online}} = r\cdot \cstar(N)$. Hence $r\le 1 + \delta_{i^{\star}}/\cstar(N) = r^{\ast\ast}$, contradicting the hypothesis $r>r^{\ast\ast}$.
\end{proof}

Because $\min_i \delta_i$ is typically small ($\delta_i=0$ corresponds to $\{i\},N\setminus\{i\}$ partitioning the grand tour with no overhead), $r^{\ast\ast}$ is close to $1$ and thus an operationally informative threshold. We stress that Theorem~\ref{thm:empty-core} is tight only \emph{within the complement-coalition mechanism}: an intermediate-size coalition $S$ with $|S|\in\{2,\ldots,n-2\}$ can independently violate the Core inequality $\sum_{i\in S} x_i \le c(S)$. Section~\ref{sec:experiments} exhibits seven such instances with $r \le r^{\ast\ast}$ whose Core is nonetheless empty.

\begin{corollary}[Fully sequential arrivals disarm Theorem~\ref{thm:empty-core}]
\label{cor:cor51}
If arrivals are fully sequential in the sense of Lemma~\ref{lem:lemma52}, then Theorem~\ref{thm:empty-core} is vacuous: no $N\setminus\{i\}$ is feasible, so its hypothesis cannot hold.
\end{corollary}

\begin{proof}
Direct from Lemma~\ref{lem:lemma52} and the hypothesis of Theorem~\ref{thm:empty-core}.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}[Near-complement mechanism (empirically observed)]
\label{rem:near-complement}
The sufficient condition $r > r^{\ast\ast}$ of Theorem~\ref{thm:empty-core} exploits only complement coalitions $N\setminus\{i\}$. Empirically, we observe instances where Theorem~\ref{thm:empty-core} is vacuous ($r\le r^{\ast\ast}$) yet the Temporal Core is nonetheless empty; in each such instance, the binding constraints in the Core LP include not only a complement coalition (size $n-1$) but also a near-complement coalition (size $n-2$) simultaneously. Section~\ref{ssec:cor16} presents quantitative evidence, and Appendix~\ref{app:restricted-lp} details the restricted-LP methodology used to certify Core emptiness at scales where enumeration of $2^n$ coalitions is infeasible. A tight sufficient condition incorporating near-complement coalitions remains open; we conjecture (Section~\ref{sec:conclusion}) that it involves balanced collections of $(n-1)$- and $(n-2)$-size coalitions.
\end{remark}

\begin{theorem}[Asymptotic tightening of $r^{\ast\ast}$]
\label{thm:asymptotic}
Assume customer coordinates $x_1,\ldots,x_n$ are drawn i.i.d.\ from the uniform distribution on $[0,L]^2$, with the depot placed at the origin $(0,0)$. Suppose the arrival pattern is such that $N\setminus\{i\}\in\F$ for at least one index $i$. Then
\[
r^{\ast\ast} \;=\; 1 + \frac{\min_{\,i:\,N\setminus\{i\}\in\F} \delta_i}{\cstar(N)}\;\xrightarrow{\text{a.s.}}\; 1 \quad \text{as } n\to\infty,
\]
with rate $r^{\ast\ast}-1 = O(n^{-1/2})$ almost surely, uniformly over the arrival patterns satisfying the hypothesis.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Fix any index $i^{0}$ with $N\setminus\{i^{0}\}\in\F$. We bound $\delta_{i^{0}}$ first, then scale by $\cstar(N)$.

\emph{Step 1: insertion and monotonicity bounds.}
Inserting the detour $\text{depot}\to x_{i^{0}}\to\text{depot}$ into any tour of $N\setminus\{i^{0}\}$ shows
\begin{equation}\label{eq:insert}
\cstar(N) \;\le\; c(N\setminus\{i^{0}\}) + c(\{i^{0}\}),
\end{equation}
whence $\cstar(N) - c(N\setminus\{i^{0}\}) \le c(\{i^{0}\}) = 2\,d(0,x_{i^{0}})$.
Conversely, removing $x_{i^{0}}$ from the optimal tour on $N$ and short-cutting its two tour neighbors yields a valid Hamiltonian cycle on $N\setminus\{i^{0}\}\cup\{0\}$ of no greater length, by the triangle inequality~\citep[\S 2.1]{steele1997probability}; hence $c(N\setminus\{i^{0}\}) \le \cstar(N)$ and $\cstar(N) - c(N\setminus\{i^{0}\}) \ge 0$. Combining:
\[
0 \;\le\; \delta_{i^{0}} \;=\; c(\{i^{0}\}) - \bigl[\,\cstar(N) - c(N\setminus\{i^{0}\})\bigr] \;\le\; c(\{i^{0}\}) \;=\; 2\,d(0,x_{i^{0}}).
\]
Since $x_{i^{0}}\in[0,L]^2$, $d(0,x_{i^{0}})\le L\sqrt{2}$ and thus $\delta_{i^{0}} \le 2L\sqrt{2} = O(1)$, independent of the arrival pattern.

\emph{Step 2: BHH lower bound on $\cstar(N)$.} By the Beardwood--Halton--Hammersley theorem \citep{beardwood1959shortest,steele1981subadditive}, the length of the optimal Hamiltonian cycle through $n$ i.i.d.\ uniform points in $[0,L]^2$ satisfies
\[
\frac{\cstar(N)}{\sqrt{n}} \;\xrightarrow{\text{a.s.}}\; \beta_{\mathrm{BHH}}\,L \quad \text{as } n\to\infty,
\]
for a universal constant $\beta_{\mathrm{BHH}}>0$. Adding a fixed depot shifts the length by $O(1)$, which is absorbed into the $o(\sqrt{n})$ term; hence $\cstar(N) = \Theta(\sqrt{n})$ a.s.

\emph{Step 3: ratio.} Combining Steps~1--2,
\[
r^{\ast\ast}-1 \;=\; \frac{\min_{\,i:\,N\setminus\{i\}\in\F}\delta_i}{\cstar(N)} \;\le\; \frac{\delta_{i^{0}}}{\cstar(N)} \;\le\; \frac{2L\sqrt{2}}{\beta_{\mathrm{BHH}}\,L\,\sqrt{n}\,(1+o(1))} \;=\; O(n^{-1/2}) \quad\text{a.s.}
\]
The numerator bound from Step~1 uses only the fixed coordinates $x_{i^{0}}\in[0,L]^2$ and not the arrival pattern, so the rate holds uniformly across patterns meeting the hypothesis. Non-negativity $r^{\ast\ast}-1\ge 0$ from Step~1 completes the claim.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}[Faster decay when a depot-close complement is feasible]
\label{rem:asymptotic-tighter}
Let $i^{\star}\in\arg\min_i d(0,x_i)$ denote the customer closest to the depot. Under i.i.d.\ uniform sampling on $[0,L]^2$, the minimum of $n$ points' distances to the origin satisfies $\min_i d(0,x_i) = \Theta(n^{-1/2})$ almost surely (by standard extreme-value arguments; the radial density of uniform points near the depot satisfies $p(r)\propto r$ as $r\to 0$, whether the depot sits inside the square or at its corner, yielding the same $n^{-1/2}$ rate up to a constant). If, in addition, $N\setminus\{i^{\star}\}\in\F$---a condition automatically met when all customers share a waiting window, and more broadly whenever the arrival pattern admits the depot-closest customer's complement---Step~1 of the proof applied to $i^{\star}$ yields $\delta_{i^{\star}} \le 2\,d(0,x_{i^{\star}}) = \Theta(n^{-1/2})$ a.s., hence
\[
r^{\ast\ast}-1 \;=\; \frac{\min_i \delta_i}{\cstar(N)} \;\le\; \frac{\delta_{i^{\star}}}{\cstar(N)} \;=\; O(n^{-1}) \quad\text{a.s.,}
\]
a faster rate than the generic $O(n^{-1/2})$ of Theorem~\ref{thm:asymptotic}.

Operationally, the faster $O(n^{-1})$ rate applies primarily to arrival patterns close to the simultaneous regime, where Theorem~\ref{thm:empty-core}'s hypothesis is easily met. In sparser regimes (sequential patterns, large queue gaps) $r^{\ast\ast}$ is not defined in the first place---no $N\setminus\{i\}$ need be feasible---and the Remark is vacuous; Corollary~\ref{cor:cor52}'s structural obstruction governs those regimes.
\end{remark}

\subsection{Conditions under which the Core survives}
\label{ssec:survive}

\begin{theorem}[Inheritance of Static Core stability]
\label{thm:core-inheritance}
If $C(N)_{\text{online}} = \cstar(N)$, i.e.\ the online tour is optimal, then every static-TSG Core allocation remains in the Temporal Core; in particular $\mathrm{Core}_{\text{static}}(N,c)\subseteq\mathrm{Core}(N,c,\F)$.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Let $x\in\mathrm{Core}_{\text{static}}$. Efficiency is inherited since $\sum_i x_i = c(N) = \cstar(N) = C(N)_{\text{online}}$. For any $S\in\F\subseteq 2^N\setminus\{\emptyset\}$, the static constraint $\sum_{i\in S} x_i\le c(S)$ is already imposed on $x$, so it also holds in the temporal setting.
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}[Structural obstruction of the complement mechanism]
\label{cor:cor52}
If the peak waiting queue satisfies $k=\max_t |U_t| < n-1$, then no coalition of the form $N\setminus\{i\}$ is feasible and Theorem~\ref{thm:empty-core} cannot fire. The only remaining \emph{a priori} mechanism for emptying the Temporal Core under $k<n-1$ is Remark~\ref{rem:remark1}, which is dominated by $r^{\ast}$ and rarely binds in practice. Intermediate-coalition violations remain logically possible; their empirical frequency is characterized in Section~\ref{sec:experiments}.
\end{corollary}

\begin{proof}
If $N\setminus\{i\}\in\F$ for some $i$, then at some instant $t$ the queue $U_t$ contains all $j\neq i$, so $|U_t|\ge n-1$ and $k\ge n-1$, contradicting $k<n-1$. The second claim follows from Theorem~\ref{thm:empty-core} and Remarks~\ref{rem:remark1}--\ref{rem:rstar}.
\end{proof}

\subsection{Discussion}
\label{ssec:discussion}

Parameters interact as follows.
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.5em]
\item The \emph{tight} threshold $r^{\ast\ast}$ is sensitive only to the cheapest complement coalition: a single pair $(i,N\setminus\{i\})\in\F$ with small $\delta_i$ is enough to expose the Core.
\item The structural parameter $k$ bounds queue depth. Since $N\setminus\{i\}$-feasibility requires $|U_t|\ge n-1$, the inequality $k<n-1$ is exactly the combinatorial negation of Theorem~\ref{thm:empty-core}'s hypothesis.
\item Arrival patterns interpolate between two extremes: simultaneous ($k=n$, $\F=2^N\setminus\{\emptyset\}$, classical TSG) and fully sequential ($k\le 1$ in the steady state, Theorem~\ref{thm:empty-core} vacuous).
\end{itemize}
In the language of queueing theory, if customer inter-arrival rate $\lambda$ exceeds service throughput by enough that $k\ge n-1$ with positive probability, the Core is at risk; otherwise it is safe. This provides an actionable design lever: dispatchers that enforce short promise times (thereby serving orders before too many accumulate) are automatically promoting stable cost allocation.

\section{Computational Experiments}
\label{sec:experiments}

We report a computational study whose purpose is to (i) verify that the theoretical conditions fire exactly as predicted, (ii) quantify how much sharper $r^{\ast\ast}$ is than $r^{\ast}$, and (iii) characterize the empirical frequency of Core existence across realistic arrival regimes.

\subsection{Design}
\label{ssec:design}

\paragraph{Dimensionless framework.} Instances follow the standard dimensionless normalization of \citet{steele1997probability}: customers are sampled uniformly in $[0,L]^2$ with $L=\sqrt{n}$, fixing the customer density at one per unit area. Vehicle speed is normalized to one (unit distance per unit time), and the depot is at the origin. The system is governed by a single dimensionless load parameter
\[
  \rho \;=\; \frac{L/v}{\tau} \;=\; \frac{\text{characteristic travel time}}{\text{mean inter-arrival interval}},
\]
where $\tau$ is the mean inter-arrival interval of a given pattern. All theoretical bounds and empirical statistics depend on $(n,\rho)$ alone; the joint rescaling $(L,v,\tau)\to(\alpha L,\alpha v,\alpha\tau)$ for any $\alpha>0$ leaves all observables invariant. We verify this invariance empirically to machine precision in Appendix~\ref{app:scale-invariance}.

\paragraph{Arrival patterns.} Seven arrival patterns span the $\rho$-spectrum:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \emph{A} ($\rho=\infty$): all customers arrive simultaneously at $t=0$.
  \item \emph{$\text{B}_{\text{heavy}}$} ($\rho=4$), \emph{$\text{B}_{\text{medium}}$} ($\rho=2$), \emph{$\text{B}_{\text{light}}$} ($\rho=0.5$): sequential arrivals at uniform interval $\tau=\beta_{\mathrm{BHH}}/\rho$, where $\beta_{\mathrm{BHH}}\approx 0.7124$ is the Beardwood--Halton--Hammersley constant.
  \item \emph{C} ($\rho=2$): clustered arrivals in two angular cohorts, interleaved in space.
  \item \emph{D} ($\rho=1$): reverse-angle zig-zag order.
  \item \emph{E} ($\rho=1$): uniformly random arrivals in $[0,n\tau]$.
\end{itemize}
Patterns $\text{B}_{\text{medium}}$ and $\text{C}$ share $\rho=2$ but differ in arrival structure; Section~\ref{ssec:pattern} shows that this structural difference alone suffices to place them in qualitatively distinct regimes of Core stability, illustrating that the load parameter $\rho$ is necessary but not sufficient to characterize dynamics.

\paragraph{Computational setup.} The online tour is computed by the nearest-neighbor heuristic; Section~\ref{ssec:policy} compares this baseline with cheapest-insertion and batch re-optimization policies. The static TSP $\cstar(S)$ for each $S$ is computed exactly by dynamic programming (Held--Karp) for $|S|\le 15$ and by the LKH heuristic \citep{helsgaun2000effective} otherwise. The Temporal Nucleolus is computed by the sequential LP~\eqref{eq:lp-q} implemented with PuLP and the CBC solver.

Table~\ref{tab:design} summarizes the design: 7 patterns $\times$ 5 sizes $\times$ 5 seeds $=$ 175 instances per dispatch policy, and 3 policies yield 525 policy-instance pairs in the main study, plus an additional scale-up grid at $n\in\{20,30,50\}$ reported in Section~\ref{ssec:scaleup}.

\subsection{Verification of Corollary~\ref{cor:cor52}}
\label{ssec:cor16}

\paragraph{Structural obstruction.} Partitioning the 175 main-study instances (NN policy) by peak queue regime yields three strata (Figure~\ref{fig:core-k}): 79 instances with $k<n-1$ all have nonempty Core ($100\%$); 16 instances with $k=n-1$ also all have nonempty Core ($100\%$); and 59 of 80 instances with $k=n$ retain a nonempty Core ($73.8\%$). The structural obstruction of Corollary~\ref{cor:cor52} holds without exception: whenever the peak queue depth stays below $n-1$, the Temporal Core is nonempty. All empty Cores occur in the saturated regime $k=n$, consistent with the requirement that at least one complement coalition $N\setminus\{i\}$ be feasible. A per-pattern breakdown across the size grid $n\in\{5,7,10,12,15\}$ is deferred to Appendix~\ref{app:aux} (Figure~\ref{fig:core-n}).

\paragraph{Decomposition of empty Cores ($k=n$ regime).} Of the 21 empty-Core instances in the $k=n$ regime, Theorem~\ref{thm:empty-core} accounts for 14 via its complement-coalition certificate ($r>r^{\ast\ast}$). The remaining 7 satisfy $r\le r^{\ast\ast}$---Theorem~\ref{thm:empty-core} is vacuous---yet the Core is empty. We refer to this as the \emph{near-complement regime} (Remark~\ref{rem:near-complement}) and characterize it below.

\paragraph{Near-complement mechanism (quantitative evidence).} Consider the instance (Pattern A, $n=30$, seed 123), representative of the seven near-complement cases in the main grid and of the scale-up observations in Section~\ref{ssec:scaleup}. At this instance the NN policy yields $r=1.038<r^{\ast\ast}=1.088$, so Theorem~\ref{thm:empty-core} is vacuous. However, a restricted Core LP over 10{,}000 sampled coalitions---including all singletons, all complements $N\setminus\{i\}$, and random intermediate-size subsets---is \emph{infeasible} with optimal nucleolus excess $\varepsilon^{\ast}=0.662>0$, certifying that the Temporal Core is empty. The binding constraints at the LP optimum include both size-$(n-1)$ (complement) and size-$(n-2)$ (near-complement) coalitions simultaneously, indicating that no single complement violation can certify emptiness, but the joint effect of complement and near-complement constraints does. The same pattern recurs at $n=20$ ($\varepsilon^{\ast}=0.765$) and $n=50$ ($\varepsilon^{\ast}=12.4$) for seed 123 under Pattern A, with binding sizes $\{n-2,n-1\}$ in each case. By contrast, a clean Theorem~\ref{thm:empty-core} case (Pattern A, $n=30$, seed 42, $r>r^{\ast\ast}$) binds only at size $n-1$. Appendix~\ref{app:restricted-lp} details the methodology and reports the full case studies.

\paragraph{Interpretation.} Theorem~\ref{thm:empty-core} and Corollary~\ref{cor:cor52} together characterize two of three mechanisms affecting Temporal Core stability: the complement-coalition mechanism (Theorem~\ref{thm:empty-core}) and the structural obstruction at $k<n-1$ (Corollary~\ref{cor:cor52}). The remaining seven main-grid instances---and the seed-123 scaleup cases at $n\in\{20,30,50\}$---reveal a third, near-complement, mechanism (Remark~\ref{rem:near-complement}), whose analytic characterization we leave open.

A noteworthy secondary observation: the mean competitive ratio $\bar r$ rises sharply in the heavily sequential regimes ($\text{B}_{\text{light}}$: $1.71$, E: $1.41$, D: $1.33$) because the online tour takes detours to reach customers as they arrive while $\cstar(N)$ reflects the offline-optimal route. Despite this large $\bar r$, the empirical Core-existence rate stays at $100\%$ for these patterns---a direct consequence of $|\F|/(2^n-1)$ being small. This is the operational content of Corollary~\ref{cor:cor52} and of the broader sequential-pattern argument of Section~\ref{ssec:discussion}: when the queue stays shallow, a high $\bar r$ does not jeopardize Core stability because the complement-coalition mechanism is structurally blocked, irrespective of how much the online policy must detour to reach staggered arrivals.

\paragraph{Load parameter $\rho$ alone does not determine Core stability.} Patterns $\text{B}_{\text{medium}}$ and C share the same load parameter $\rho=2$ yet exhibit qualitatively different behavior. For $\text{B}_{\text{medium}}$ (uniform sequential arrivals), Theorem~\ref{thm:empty-core} is applicable in only $7/25$ instances and fires in $0$ of them (Core rate $100\%$). For C (two angularly-interleaved batches), Theorem~\ref{thm:empty-core} is applicable in $24/25$ instances and fires in $3/25$ (Core rate $88\%$). The structural difference in the arrival geometry alone accounts for this gap. This confirms that $\rho$, while necessary for placing a pattern on the saturation spectrum, is not sufficient to characterize Core stability: the spatial-temporal structure of the arrival process matters independently.

\subsection{\texorpdfstring{Sharpness of $r^{\ast\ast}$ versus $r^{\ast}$}{Sharpness of r** versus r*}}

Figure~\ref{fig:rhist} contrasts the distributions of the complement-mechanism threshold $r^{\ast\ast}$ (Theorem~\ref{thm:empty-core}) and the classical bound $r^{\ast}$ (Remark~\ref{rem:rstar}). Empirically, $\bar r^{\ast\ast}=1.355$ versus $\bar r^{\ast}=4.351$, a factor of $3.21$. Because the realized ratio $\bar r = 1.326$ is close to $r^{\ast\ast}$ but far below $r^{\ast}$, Theorem~\ref{thm:empty-core} is operationally informative whereas Remark~\ref{rem:rstar} almost never fires. The classical bound grows with $n$ because $r^{\ast} = \sum_i c(\{i\})/\cstar(N)$ has a numerator scaling linearly in $n$ while $\cstar(N)$ scales like $\sqrt{n}$ on uniform planar instances; $r^{\ast\ast}$, in contrast, is pinned near $1$ because the cheapest complement pair $(\{i\}, N\setminus\{i\})$ nearly partitions the grand tour. This explains why the gap between the two widens with $n$ and quantifies the practical advance of the temporal analysis.

A secondary point worth noting: $r^{\ast}$ depends only on coordinates---it equals $\sum_i c(\{i\})/\cstar(N)$, and neither the numerator nor the denominator sees arrivals---so under our uniform-square sampling $\bar r^{\ast}$ has essentially no variance across patterns. The operational shortfall of $r^{\ast}$ is therefore structural: it is blind to the online arrival process altogether.

\paragraph{Finding 1: $\bar r^{\ast\ast}$ decays with $n$, confirming Theorem~\ref{thm:asymptotic}.} For pattern A, $\bar r^{\ast\ast}$ drops from $1.181$ at $n=15$ (Table~\ref{tab:pattern}) to $1.119$, $1.096$, $1.068$ at $n=20, 30, 50$, respectively. Pattern C exhibits the same decay ($\text{B}_{\text{medium}}$ has no feasible complement at any scale, so $\bar r^{\ast\ast}$ is undefined). The observed rate of decrease is consistent with the $O(n^{-1/2})$ bound of Theorem~\ref{thm:asymptotic} (and within the $O(n^{-1})$ improvement of Remark~\ref{rem:asymptotic-tighter} for the simultaneous-arrival case A).

\paragraph{Finding 2: Pattern C undergoes an empirical phase transition between $n=15$ and $n=20$.} At $n=15$ the clustered pattern had an $88\%$ Core-existence rate with $\bar r = 1.199 < \bar r^{\ast\ast} = 1.363$ (Table~\ref{tab:pattern}). By $n=20$ the two quantities have crossed: $\bar r = 1.335 > \bar r^{\ast\ast} = 1.119$, and all 5 test instances have an empty Core; the same holds at $n=30, 50$. Two concurrent effects drive the transition: $\bar r$ increases (the NN competitive ratio degrades with more customers and the clustered zig-zag accumulates realized waiting), while $\bar r^{\ast\ast}$ decreases by Theorem~\ref{thm:asymptotic}. Clustered patterns are thus Core-stable only at modest $n$.

\paragraph{Finding 3: Pattern $\text{B}_{\text{medium}}$ remains Core-stable at every scale.} Across $n\in\{20,30,50\}$, $\text{B}_{\text{medium}}$ exhibits $\bar r \in [1.31,1.39]$ yet every instance retains a nonempty Core. Structurally, $\text{B}_{\text{medium}}$'s sparse sequential arrivals keep $\bar k$ strictly below $n-1$ at every scale ($\bar k = 15.4, 23.2, 37.6$ at $n=20, 30, 50$), so every complement coalition $N\setminus\{i\}$ is infeasible, Theorem~\ref{thm:empty-core} is vacuous, and Corollary~\ref{cor:cor52}'s obstruction applies. This is the most vivid validation of the ``small $k$, safe Core'' principle at scale: Core stability does not require low $r$---it requires a shallow queue.

\paragraph{Finding 4: Near-complement mechanism at seed 123.} At seed 123 under Pattern~A, Theorem~\ref{thm:empty-core} is vacuous at $n=20$ and $n=30$ ($r=1.077<r^{\ast\ast}=1.112$ and $r=1.038<r^{\ast\ast}=1.088$ respectively) but fires at $n=50$ ($r=1.306>r^{\ast\ast}=1.081$), consistent with the $O(n^{-1/2})$ decay of $\bar r^{\ast\ast}$. Nevertheless, a restricted Core LP over $10{,}000$ sampled coalitions certifies Core emptiness at all three scales, with binding constraints spanning both complement (size $n-1$) and near-complement (size $n-2$) coalitions simultaneously in each case. By contrast, a clean Theorem-\ref{thm:empty-core}-fires instance (Pattern A, $n=30$, seed 42) binds only at size $n-1$ (Section~\ref{ssec:cor16}, Appendix~\ref{app:restricted-lp}). The persistence of size-$(n-2)$ binding across scale---even where Theorem~\ref{thm:empty-core} is applicable---suggests that the near-complement mechanism is a stable structural feature of this configuration rather than a small-$n$ artifact.

\paragraph{Intractability of pattern A at $n\ge 30$.} For pattern A, $|\F| = 2^n-1$, so the Core LP has $\sim\!10^9$ and $\sim\!10^{15}$ constraints at $n=30$ and $n=50$ respectively, well beyond enumeration. Theorem~\ref{thm:empty-core} nevertheless certifies Core emptiness \emph{a priori} in $12$ of the $15$ seed--size combinations: every complement coalition is feasible, so $r^{\ast\ast}$ is well-defined, and numerically $r>r^{\ast\ast}$ holds in $3/5$ seeds at $n=20$, $4/5$ at $n=30$, and $5/5$ at $n=50$ (Table~\ref{tab:scaleup}). The three exceptions---seeds 99 and 123 at $n=20$, and seed 123 at $n=30$---have $r\le r^{\ast\ast}$; for these the restricted Core LP of Appendix~\ref{app:restricted-lp} certifies emptiness (Finding~4 above). The intractability of pattern A therefore itself \emph{demonstrates} the $k<n-1$ versus $k=n$ divide identified in Corollary~\ref{cor:cor52}: the regime where the Core LP becomes infeasible to enumerate is precisely the regime where Theorem~\ref{thm:empty-core}---supplemented by the near-complement certificate at the few exceptional seeds---can certify emptiness without enumeration.

\paragraph{Summary.} The qualitative findings of the earlier subsections persist up to $n=50$, with one refinement: the tail of Core-empty clustered instances at $n=15$ expands to near-certain Core emptiness by $n=20$ because $r^{\ast\ast}$ tightens with $n$ (Theorem~\ref{thm:asymptotic}). Corollary~\ref{cor:cor52}'s safeguard, in contrast, only strengthens with $n$: across every scale-up cell, whenever $\bar k<n-1$ the Core is nonempty irrespective of the realized competitive ratio (observed $\bar r$ as high as $1.39$ at $n=50$ for $\text{B}_{\text{medium}}$).

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

We introduced the Online Traveling Salesman Game, in which coalitions are restricted to those realizable under the online arrival process, and defined the Temporal Nucleolus as the lexicographic excess minimizer over this restricted family. We showed that the classical (static) TSG is recovered at the boundary case of simultaneous arrivals, that the Temporal Core is empty whenever the competitive ratio exceeds a complement-mechanism threshold $r^{\ast\ast}$, and that a bounded waiting queue ($k<n-1$) structurally blocks the complement-coalition empty-Core mechanism (Corollary~\ref{cor:cor52}), with empirical Core nonemptiness in $79$ of $79$ such instances. Numerical experiments over 175 instances confirm every theoretical prediction and show that this threshold is sharper than the classical bound by a factor of $3.21$, making it the first operationally informative threshold for cost-sharing stability in online routing \emph{within the complement-coalition mechanism}; the broader landscape includes near-complement effects (Section~\ref{ssec:cor16}, Remark~\ref{rem:near-complement}, Appendix~\ref{app:restricted-lp}) that warrant further study.

\paragraph{Platform-design implication.} From a platform-design perspective, our results offer suggestive evidence that enforcing short service-time promises---equivalently, keeping the peak queue depth below $n-1$---structurally rules out the complement-coalition empty-Core mechanism. Near-complement violations remain logically possible in the saturated regime and are empirically observed in a minority of instances (Section~\ref{ssec:cor16}).

\paragraph{Limitations and future work.} Four limitations merit explicit discussion.

\emph{(i) Dispatch policy.} Theorems~\ref{thm:empty-core} and~\ref{thm:core-inheritance} and Corollary~\ref{cor:cor52} use only the realized tuple $(r, \F, k)$ and are therefore policy-independent in their statements. Section~\ref{ssec:policy} extends the empirical study to cheapest-insertion and batch re-optimization policies: the qualitative conclusions---Theorem~\ref{thm:empty-core}'s direction of implication and Corollary~\ref{cor:cor52}'s structural obstruction---are preserved, while the absolute value of $\bar r$ varies by policy as expected. Extending to learning-based or forecasting-aware dispatch policies remains open.

\emph{(ii) Instance scale.} Section~\ref{ssec:scaleup} extends the study to $n\in\{20,30,50\}$ for patterns A, $\text{B}_{\text{medium}}$, and C, using LKH \citep{helsgaun2000effective} for $c^{\ast}(S)$ at $n>20$ with calibration error $\eta=0.000\%$ on $n=15$ pure-distance benchmarks. Four findings emerge: $\bar r^{\ast\ast}$ decays as $O(n^{-1/2})$ (Theorem~\ref{thm:asymptotic}), pattern C undergoes a phase transition between $n=15$ and $n=20$ as $\bar r$ overtakes $\bar r^{\ast\ast}$, pattern $\text{B}_{\text{medium}}$ remains Core-stable at every scale as its queue stays shallow ($\bar k<n-1$ throughout)---a direct validation of Corollary~\ref{cor:cor52}, and the near-complement mechanism (Remark~\ref{rem:near-complement}) is observed at seed 123 across $n\in\{20,30,50\}$ even where Theorem~\ref{thm:empty-core} fires. Pattern A is Core-LP intractable for $n\ge 30$ because $|\F| = 2^n - 1$ there; emptiness is nevertheless certified \emph{a priori} by Theorem~\ref{thm:empty-core} (12 of 15 seed--size combinations) and by a restricted Core LP (Appendix~\ref{app:restricted-lp}) for the three exceptions (seeds 99 and 123 at $n=20$, and seed 123 at $n=30$). Extending to $n\ge 100$, to non-uniform geometries, and to explicit heavy-tailed queueing models remains for future work.

\emph{(iii) Intermediate-coalition mechanism.} The seven empty-Core instances with $r\le r^{\ast\ast}$ in the main grid (Section~\ref{ssec:cor16}) are not explained by Theorem~\ref{thm:empty-core}'s complement-coalition mechanism. We conjecture that a tight characterization involves a minimum over balanced collections of intermediate-size feasible coalitions (in the sense of \citealp{shapley1971core}) and leave its precise form as an open problem; see (iv) below for empirical evidence on the binding structure.

\emph{(iv) Near-complement mechanism.} The empirical evidence of Section~\ref{ssec:cor16} and the restricted-LP case studies in Appendix~\ref{app:restricted-lp} show that Theorem~\ref{thm:empty-core}'s complement-coalition characterization is asymptotically tight ($\bar r^{\ast\ast}$ decays as $O(n^{-1/2})$ per Theorem~\ref{thm:asymptotic}) but not complete at every configuration: at seed 123 under Pattern~A at $n=20, 30$, Core emptiness is certified by simultaneous binding of complement (size $n-1$) and near-complement (size $n-2$) coalitions rather than by any single complement coalition. An analytic sufficient condition capturing this joint binding is open; we conjecture it involves balanced collections of near-complement coalitions, concretizing (iii) in the specific direction suggested by the $\{n-2, n-1\}$ binding structure observed empirically.

\emph{Broader directions.} Beyond these limitations, natural extensions include the Online Vehicle Routing Game with multiple vehicles and capacity constraints, and an A3-style consistency axiom so that allocations computed across rolling-horizon windows agree on overlapping coalitions.

\section{Restricted Core LP for near-complement certification}
\label{app:restricted-lp}

This appendix details the restricted Core LP methodology used in Section~\ref{ssec:cor16} and Section~\ref{ssec:scaleup}'s Finding~4 to certify Core emptiness at scales where enumeration of all $2^n-1$ feasible coalitions is infeasible.

\paragraph{Motivation.} The full Core LP requires one inequality per feasible coalition $S\in\mathcal{F}$, i.e., up to $2^n-1$ constraints. For Pattern~A at $n=30$ this is $2^{30}-1\approx 10^9$ and at $n=50$ it is $\approx 10^{15}$, both intractable to enumerate. Yet for these instances we wish to determine whether the Temporal Core is empty, and if so, which coalition sizes provide the certifying constraints.

\paragraph{Sampling strategy.} We solve a \emph{restricted} Core LP over a sampled subset $\mathcal{S}\subseteq\mathcal{F}$ constructed as follows:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item All $n$ singletons $\{i\}$ (always feasible).
  \item All complements $N\setminus\{i\}$ that are feasible under the arrival dynamics.
  \item The grand coalition $N$.
  \item $K$ additional coalitions drawn by uniform random subset sampling of $2^N$, retaining only those that are feasible.
\end{enumerate}
We take $K=10{,}000$ in all cases reported below. Pure-distance TSP costs $c^{\ast}(S)$ for each $S\in\mathcal{S}$ are computed exactly by Held--Karp for $|S|\le 15$ and by LKH \citep{helsgaun2000effective} otherwise. The restricted LP then solves the first-stage nucleolus program
\begin{align}
  \min_{x\in\mathbb{R}^n,\ \varepsilon\in\mathbb{R}} \varepsilon \quad
  \text{s.t.} \quad
  & \sum_{i=1}^n x_i = C_N^{\text{online}},\\
  & \sum_{i\in S} x_i - c^{\ast}(S) \le \varepsilon, \quad S\in\mathcal{S},\ S\neq N,\notag
\end{align}
solved with CBC via PuLP. The Temporal Core restricted to $\mathcal{S}$ is nonempty iff $\varepsilon^{\ast}\le 0$. If $\varepsilon^{\ast}>0$, the sample $\mathcal{S}$ certifies Core emptiness (the true Core is a subset of the restricted Core and is thus also empty). The method is a \emph{one-sided} certificate: it cannot conclude nonemptiness from a restricted sample.

\paragraph{Results.} Table~\ref{tab:restricted-lp} reports five case studies: three seed-123 instances at $n\in\{20,30,50\}$ (where Theorem~\ref{thm:empty-core} is vacuous at $n=20,30$ and fires at $n=50$), and two control instances where Theorem~\ref{thm:empty-core} fires. In all five cases, $\varepsilon^{\ast}>0$, certifying Core emptiness. For each case, we also record the sizes of the binding constraints at the LP optimum---i.e., the $S$ for which $\sum_{i\in S}x_i^{\ast}-c^{\ast}(S)=\varepsilon^{\ast}$ within numerical tolerance $10^{-4}$.

\paragraph{Interpretation.} The single clean Theorem~\ref{thm:empty-core} case in the table (seed $42$, $n=30$) binds only at size $n-1$, consistent with the theorem's single-complement certificate. All other rows exhibit simultaneous binding at sizes $n-1$ and $n-2$. For seed 123 at $n=20,30$, Theorem~\ref{thm:empty-core} is vacuous ($r\le r^{\ast\ast}$, i.e., no single complement coalition certifies emptiness), yet the combined complement-and-near-complement block of constraints does rule out every feasible Core allocation. For seed 123 at $n=50$, Theorem~\ref{thm:empty-core} does fire; nevertheless, size-$(n-2)$ constraints remain binding, indicating that the near-complement mechanism persists into the asymptotic regime where the complement mechanism also applies. This motivates the conjecture in Section~\ref{sec:conclusion}: that a tight sufficient condition for Core emptiness beyond Theorem~\ref{thm:empty-core} involves balanced collections of $(n-1)$- and $(n-2)$-size coalitions in the sense of \citet{shapley1971core}.
